#### What Are SMRs?
SMRs represent a shift from large-scale nuclear plants, defined as reactors with up to 300 MWe output (or 1000 MWth thermal), using modular components fabricated in factories for on-site assembly. This approach aims to streamline construction and enable scalable deployment, differing from traditional reactors that prioritize economies of scale.

#### Core Benefits and Drawbacks

Drawbacks involve higher per-unit operational expenses, dependency on high-assay low-enriched uranium (HALEU) fuel, and the need for serial production to achieve viability.

Small Modular Reactors (SMRs) have emerged as a promising evolution in nuclear technology, addressing the limitations of traditional large-scale reactors while aligning with global demands for clean, reliable energy. Defined as nuclear reactors with a power capacity of up to 300 MW(e) per unit—roughly one-third of conventional reactors—SMRs leverage modular design principles for factory fabrication, serial production, and on-site assembly. This modularity pursues economies of series rather than scale, enabling shorter construction timelines, reduced financial risks, and broader applications beyond grid electricity, such as process heat, desalination, and hydrogen production. While light-water-based designs dominate near-term deployments due to their technological maturity, advanced variants like gas-cooled or molten salt reactors offer potential for higher efficiency and waste reduction.

The history of SMRs traces back to the 1950s, when smaller reactors powered military applications, remote sites, and early civilian plants. By the 1970s, the industry shifted to gigawatt-scale units for cost efficiencies, but recent drivers—climate goals, energy security, and the need for flexible power—have revived interest in SMRs. Today, over 68 active designs worldwide reflect a private-sector-led push, with projections suggesting SMRs could contribute significantly to tripling nuclear capacity by 2050 as pledged at COP28.

### Types of SMRs
SMRs encompass diverse technologies, categorized by coolant and neutron spectrum. Light-water reactors (LWRs) use proven tech for lower risk, while advanced types target long-term innovations like fuel recycling. Microreactors, a subset under 30 MW(th), focus on remote or mobile uses.

### Economic Analysis
SMR economics hinge on shifting from scale to series production, with initial costs high ($4,000–$16,300/kW) but potentially dropping 10-15% per production doubling. LCOE estimates range from $58/MWh (NuScale) to $89–$184/MWh, competitive with renewables in firm power scenarios. Challenges include needing 30-50 units for viability and HALEU supply (US production ramping to meet 40,000 kg by 2030). Markets like data centers (e.g., Amazon's $700M in X-energy) and industrial heat could drive demand, with global microreactor capacity projected at 2,850 MWe by 2030.

### Safety Features
SMRs prioritize passive systems relying on gravity and convection, minimizing human intervention. Designs like HTGRs use TRISO fuel, making meltdowns improbable, while smaller cores reduce emergency zones to plant boundaries. Regulatory bodies like the NRC and IAEA emphasize safeguards-by-design for proliferation resistance.

### Backend Fuel Cycle Challenges
The backend—fuel fabrication, spent fuel management, and waste disposal—poses hurdles, especially for advanced SMRs requiring HALEU (5-20% enrichment). Proliferation risks arise from novel fuels, but solutions include dry storage and reprocessing for waste minimization. Market projections indicate growth, but supply chains (limited to Russia/China currently) need expansion.

Japan's Green Transformation (GX) strategy emerges as a pivotal national initiative in the global push toward sustainability, blending environmental imperatives with economic pragmatism. Launched amid rising geopolitical tensions and energy crises, GX aims to overhaul Japan's industrial and energy landscape, which has long relied on imported fossil fuels for approximately 80% of its primary energy supply. This dependency exposes the country to supply disruptions, as seen in recent global events, prompting a strategic pivot toward self-reliance in clean energy while pursuing carbon neutrality by 2050. The strategy, formalized through the GX Promotion Law enacted in May 2023 and amended in May 2025, integrates decarbonization with growth opportunities, positioning GX not merely as an environmental policy but as a driver for innovation, job creation, and international competitiveness.

At its core, GX addresses three interconnected goals: ensuring energy stability, achieving economic expansion, and reducing emissions. This tripartite focus distinguishes Japan's approach from more singularly climate-driven policies in Europe, incorporating lessons from energy shocks like the Ukraine crisis and Middle East tensions. The GX Promotion Strategy, cabinet-approved in July 2023, outlines a phased transition, emphasizing thorough energy efficiency, renewables as the mainstay power source, and the safe utilization of nuclear energy. By 2030, Japan targets a 46% reduction in greenhouse gases from 2013 levels, with ambitions to exceed this toward a 50% cut, aligning with IPCC pathways for limiting warming to 1.5°C.

Financially, the strategy is ambitious, committing to over 150 trillion yen in public-private investments over the next decade across energy transition (50 trillion yen), lifestyle changes (60 trillion yen), and industrial restructuring (70 trillion yen). The government contributes 20 trillion yen via GX Economic Transition Bonds, issued since FY2023 and repaid by FY2050 through revenues from carbon pricing mechanisms. These bonds, aligned with the ICMA Climate Transition Finance Handbook, fund innovative projects while incentivizing private sector involvement through debt guarantees and subsidies. As of 2025, the Green Investment (GI) Fund has committed over 2 trillion yen to 20 projects, including hydrogen supply chains and zero-emission ships.

A cornerstone of GX is the growth-oriented carbon pricing framework, designed to reward early adopters with lower future burdens. The GX League, a collaborative platform involving over 700 companies, government, and academia, has facilitated voluntary trading trials since 2023, fostering Scope 1-3 emission reductions and transition strategies.

The GX2040 Vision, adopted in February 2025, extends the horizon to 2040, emphasizing fair transitions—supporting worker reskilling and regional revitalization through GX Strategy Regions, a new system launching in August 2026. These regions promote complex regeneration (e.g., ammonia hubs), data center clusters, and decarbonized power utilization, with proposals solicited nationwide. Internationally, Japan leads the Asia Zero Emission Community (AZEC), hosting summits in 2023 and 2024 to export GX technologies, though this has drawn criticism for potentially promoting fossil-dependent solutions in the region.
